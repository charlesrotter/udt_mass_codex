# Lay readout

We found something real, but a defined set of missing choices still separates
the mathematical group from a physical law.

The simplest UDT clock/ruler part already knows how to combine pairwise. If
one comparison has reciprocal depth `phi1` and a second has `phi2`, composing
them adds the depths. That is the same clean rule that produced the
exponential dilation in the first place. Choosing a weighted average among
several different configurations is an additional choice; UDT has not yet
selected those weights or that mean.

More surprisingly, the complete ten-part metric frame used in our recent
maps also has a coherent multiplication table. Its clock/ruler block, angular
block, and four cross-connections do not combine independently; they form one
exact mathematical “gearbox.” Common scale sits in its center and commutes
with everything.

That is a promising structural clue. It means the orchestra really does have
an ensemble rule inside this complete frame description.

The remaining problem is that a metric can be written using many equivalent
frames. Our gearbox works only after choosing one common way to line those
frames up. If two equivalent descriptions are lined up differently, the
gearbox can produce a different output metric. UDT has partly lined up the
clock/ruler pair at the finite-cell seal, but it has not yet supplied the
complete angular-and-shift alignment everywhere.

So the result is:

- the reciprocal instrument has a native composition law;
- the full ten-field frame has an exact candidate group law;
- UDT has not yet shown that this candidate is independent of how we label and
  align the frames;
- the separate `phi` field still needs its own compatible composition rule.

This also explains the earlier bank-simplex disagreement. J1 and J2 were two
different ways of assembling the same endpoints. The metric did not force
either recipe. Even fixing every edge—or every boundary face—still leaves
room to alter the interior while preserving all of that boundary information.

The next conceptual target is therefore much narrower than “invent a complete
interpolation”: determine whether the metric and finite-cell structure supply
a type-correct way to compare the input frames, remove their independent
frame-label freedom, choose a weighted multi-input rule, and compose the
scalar field consistently. If they do, a large part of the angular/shift
assembly problem may already be solved.
