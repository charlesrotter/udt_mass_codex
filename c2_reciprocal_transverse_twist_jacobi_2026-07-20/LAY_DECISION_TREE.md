# Lay decision tree

## What was found?

The UDT metric itself can make one geometric twist behave as though it has two kinds of resistance:

1. a fixed positive resistance to bending the twist more sharply; and
2. a second resistance—or assistance—controlled by how the surrounding clock/ruler dilation bends.

Both came from one curvature-squared metric expression. The historical two-term particle action was
not inserted.

## Is that the missing matter law?

No. This was one local static metric wrinkle, not a three-dimensional linked object. The second term
also changes sign with the unsolved background. Until the background and twist solve one complete
problem together, the calculation cannot say that the object is stable or material.

## Does the nonround Hopfion now matter more?

Yes, as a clue. The stable finite-box configuration says the useful shape is genuinely twisted and
three-dimensional. This audit says that a genuine metric twist already receives the same two
derivative orders that were previously supplied by hand in the carrier model. The possible bridge is
therefore narrower and less arbitrary than before.

## What result would advance the route?

- If the coupled metric equations select a reciprocal background on which the second coefficient has
  the required sign and a regular global twist exists, continue toward the full three-dimensional
  finite-cell problem.
- If every admissible on-shell background makes the coefficient vanish or have the wrong sign, this
  economical metric-parent route fails in the tested branch.
- If several signs and branches remain, the missing selector is still global boundary/bootstrap
  closure, not a coefficient to be fitted.

No result here authorizes a carrier, an `S2`, a particle action, GPU grinding, or a mass claim.
