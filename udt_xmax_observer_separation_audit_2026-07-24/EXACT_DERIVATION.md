## 2. What frame sharing does and does not mean

Once \(D_g\) and its domain exist, their global diameter is one scalar
invariant; it is not a different fitted value for each observer.  That weak
statement is distinct from

\[
\sup_B D_g(A,B)=X_{\max}\qquad\hbox{for every }A.
\]

The second statement says every observer has the same maximal reach.  It
requires a transitive/homogeneous global completion or another theorem.  It
does not follow from the existence of one global supremum.

Pair exchange requires

\[
D_g(A,B)=D_g(B,A),
\]

but this is only the symmetry of a distance.  It does not supply a
fractional-linear composition law for three observers.

## 3. A maximum pair distance does not imply an edge

The following exact countermodels all admit a finite diameter:

- A closed interval has a boundary.
- An open interval has a finite supremum which is not attained.
- A circle of circumference \(2X_{\max}\) has diameter \(X_{\max}\), is
  homogeneous, and has no boundary.
- A round sphere of radius \(X_{\max}/\pi\) has geodesic diameter
  \(X_{\max}\), is homogeneous, and has no boundary.
- A Euclidean ball of radius \(X_{\max}/2\) has diameter \(X_{\max}\) and a
  boundary.
- A suitably scaled flat torus has finite diameter, is homogeneous, and has
  no boundary.

Therefore finite diameter alone selects none of: boundary, edge, preferred
center, radial direction, horizon, seam, quotient, topology, attainment, or
boundary functional.

## 5. Why the angular sector is load-bearing

Take three points in a Euclidean control geometry.  If
\(D(A,B)=a\), \(D(B,C)=b\), and the angle at \(B\) is \(\theta\), then

\[
D(A,C)^2=a^2+b^2+2ab\cos\theta .
\]

The derivative with respect to \(\theta\) is

\[
-2ab\sin\theta,
\]

which is generically nonzero.  Thus two adjacent scalar separations do not
determine the third.  A universal scalar operation
\(D(A,C)=F(D(A,B),D(B,C))\) is impossible on the complete pair geometry
without extra alignment, angular, curvature, path, or holonomy data.

The old fractional law

\[
x\oplus y={x+y\over 1+xy/X_{\max}^2}
\]

and the additive coordinate
\(X_{\max}\operatorname{atanh}(x/X_{\max})\) remain exact in the supplied
one-dimensional, oriented, collinear projective class.  They are not the
general composition law for nonnegative observer separation.
