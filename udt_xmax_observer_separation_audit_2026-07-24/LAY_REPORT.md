# Lay report

The correction is simple and important:

`X_max` is not the distance from a center to the edge of the universe.  It is
the largest possible separation between two observers.

That is like the diameter of a space, not its radius.  A circle has a largest
distance between two points but no edge.  A sphere does too.  A ball has an
edge, but its diameter is twice its radius.  Those examples prove that
knowing the maximum separation does not yet tell us the universe's shape,
whether it has an edge, or where any center would be.

This does not erase the useful WR-L work.  The WR-L metric still has its exact
local clock-slowing profile, its infinite optical reach, its regular
lightlike surface, and its SNe fit.  The correction is that the `X` in that
centered profile is not automatically the same thing as the universe-wide
two-observer `X_max`.  The full geometry must derive that connection.

It also explains why the angular sector keeps mattering.  If observer A is a
fixed distance from B, and B is a fixed distance from C, the A-to-C distance
still changes when the angle between the two legs changes.  So a simple
one-number reciprocity formula cannot describe every three-observer
configuration.  The older fractional formula remains exact for a straight,
oriented, one-dimensional comparison, but it is not yet the general law of
observer separation.

The concrete gain is that we now know the missing object precisely.  UDT must
derive from the complete metric:

1. which observer events are being compared;
2. the nonnegative separation between that pair; and
3. how the angular and global completion enter it.

Then `X_max` is the supremum of those separations.  Only afterward can we ask
whether it is unattainable, whether every observer sees the same limiting
reach, whether it matches the WR-L scale, or whether bootstrap mass/density
selects its value.
