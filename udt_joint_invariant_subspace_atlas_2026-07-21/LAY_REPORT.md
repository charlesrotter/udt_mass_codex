# Lay Report

We asked whether the nine named pointwise families in this atlas naturally divide the local
geometry into two special pairs of directions—without first telling them which directions we wanted.

Think of the metric, curvature, and phi field as an orchestra. Earlier maps showed that the
instruments interact. This audit asked a sharper question: does the music itself divide the
orchestra into two uniquely identifiable sections?

Across 6,144 local metric configurations, the corrected answer has two layers.

Individual sections really do organize themselves. Ricci curvature, the phi Hessian, and some of
their smaller combinations repeatedly divide the four directions into one real two-direction plane
and its two-direction complement. We found 11,983 such family cases. A fresh reviewer caught that
our first classifier had missed them because it treated a complex-conjugate mathematical pair as an
obstruction instead of combining it into a real plane.

Then the two registered full curvature-plus-phi families enter. When all of their curvature
directions play together with phi, every one of those smaller-family partitions is mixed back into
the four-dimensional space. In 5,760 configurations no nontrivial common section survives; in the
remaining 384 the named full families are too degenerate to choose one.

Three other possible shortcuts still fail inside this map:

- Following the direction in which phi changes never grew into exactly a two-dimensional section.
- Curvature never supplied one clean real plane through a unique simple eigen-pattern.
- Some smaller subsets produced multiple competing pairings. Others did produce a unique
  family-level pairing, but none of those pairings survived either registered full family.

We also rewrote a large independent sample in nonlinear coordinates. The underlying classifications
stayed the same to machine precision. A first numerical classifier falsely reported coordinate
changes because it chose arbitrary axes inside a degenerate space; that error was preserved,
diagnosed, fixed, and independently checked.

The useful insight is not “there is no angular geometry.” It is more interesting:

> Some instruments naturally make a `2+2` section, but neither registered full curvature-plus-phi
> family leaves that section alone. Within these named families, the full symphony is irreducibly
> four-dimensional.

That is a concrete clue: a physical angular sector, if UDT has one, is unlikely to be a pointwise
subspace preserved by every local curvature operator. It may instead be a transported, averaged, or
globally compatible pattern across a finite cell or time-live history. Those possibilities remain
open. This audit did not choose an action, boundary, carrier, or new mechanism.
