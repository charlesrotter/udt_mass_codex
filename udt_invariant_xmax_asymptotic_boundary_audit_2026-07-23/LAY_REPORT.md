# Lay readout

Yes, treating `Xmax` like the distance-version of Einsteinian `c` helps.

It tells us the edge need not be a physical wall that something can stand
on. It can be a real universal scale that every observer agrees about but
that no ordinary observer ever reaches. This removes our earlier need for
two ordinary “end seals.”

The existing WR-L metric already behaves this way. As its outer limit is
approached:

- the available coordinate distance stays finite;
- the proper distance stays finite;
- the clock factor falls toward zero; and
- the metric depth needed to reach it becomes infinite.

That is strong geometric support for the analogy.

What it does not tell us is how to number the positions between the center
and the limit. Several rulers can mark the same interval differently while
all agreeing on zero and `Xmax`.

The projective reciprocal ruler gives `tanh(phi)`. The WR-L coordinate ruler
gives `1-exp(-2phi)`. Its proper-distance ruler gives `1-exp(-phi)`. They
all approach the same edge, but they disagree everywhere inside.

So the remaining question is not whether `Xmax` can be a meaningful
universal limit. It can. The question is which metric measurement the word
“distance” refers to.

The angular problem remains separate. Making the positional limit
universal does not force the angular sector to use exactly unit reciprocal
weight. That remaining number, `lambda`, is still open.
