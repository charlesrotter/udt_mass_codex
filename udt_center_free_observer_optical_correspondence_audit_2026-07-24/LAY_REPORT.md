# Lay report

We found a more complete version of the “orchestra.”

When one observer looks toward another, the metric has to do at least two
different jobs:

1. compare their clocks along the direction between them;
2. describe what happens sideways—how a tiny bundle of neighboring
   directions spreads, focuses, twists, or crosses.

The second job controls apparent area and image size. The metric really does
contain that information. But there can be several valid connecting paths,
so the answer can be a family rather than one number.

The two best existing UDT geometries each contain half the desired whole:

- The round geometry treats every observer equally and has a complete
  sideways-area law, but its clocks do not dilate.
- WR-L has the desired clock dilation and the area law that produced the good
  supernova result, but only in a chart centered on one observer. That chart
  cannot yet be turned into one smooth universe where every observer is
  equivalent.

So the supernova result is not erased. It remains a strong clue about the
correct local observer-to-observer readout. What is still missing is the
global rule that lets every observer use that readout consistently.

The next target is therefore not another guessed distance curve. It is the
single geometric object that carries both the clock comparison and the
sideways area transport between arbitrary observers.
