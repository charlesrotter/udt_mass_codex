# Lay decision tree

1. **Are redshift, time dilation, and effective light speed related?**  
   Yes, conditionally. In UDT's reciprocal metric they are readings of the same dilation field.

2. **Are they literally the same number?**  
   No. Clock rate carries one power of the lapse; reciprocal optical speed carries two because the ruler changes inversely.

3. **Does time dilation alone change light's coordinate speed?**  
   Not uniquely. The answer also depends on what happens to rulers. The same clock slowdown can produce `c0 N`, `c0`, or `c0 N^2`.

4. **Does local light speed change?**  
   A metric-normalized observer still assigns `c0` to metric-null curves. A material-clock theorem and any scale-dependent local cone remain open.

5. **Does cosmological redshift prove that mass increases—or that mass is fundamentally constant?**  
   Neither. In a standard fixed-mass comparison, static redshift lowers the remotely assigned or received energy. Native rest, inertial, and gravitational mass behavior still needs dynamics and material coupling.

6. **How could UDT differ at tiny and enormous distances but match GR in between?**  
   An operational scale law could have a negative-phi UV branch, a broad near-zero ordinary plateau, and a positive-phi IR branch. The calculation proves only the exact neutral anchor, not the breadth of that plateau.

7. **Can the existing metric already do that?**  
   Not as literal different cones at one event. Those require multiscale geometry. Effective optics might instead use one metric plus a scale-dependent observer/readout or nonlocal travel rule. UDT must select which and define operational scale.

8. **What if the adapted light slope never exceeds `c0`?**  
   Then `c0` is its pinned coordinate-slope ceiling and normalized optical stretch has a floor of one. Neither statement is yet an invariant material signal bound.

9. **What is the next conceptual task?**  
   Preserve the open observer/line/representative pins, then derive an operational scale selector, the geometric/readout law that uses it, endpoint comparison, and any claimed material/causal coupling before choosing a profile.
