# Lay decision tree

## What was tested?

Instead of demanding that the geometry preserve four individual directions, preserve only the
two-dimensional clock-and-distance sheet and its two-dimensional angular complement.

## Does that work mathematically?

Yes, but only for a definite class of metrics.

- Each sheet must fit together into actual local surfaces; it cannot twist through the other sheet.
- Crossing from one sheet into the other may enlarge or shrink the whole sheet uniformly.
- It may not squeeze one direction while stretching the other.

When those conditions hold, there is exactly one tested conformal connection that preserves the
split.

## Does the familiar UDT reciprocal metric pass?

Yes. Its clock and ruler can dilate reciprocally by any profile, and its angular sphere can have any
positive radius and retain its curvature. The law does not choose the dilation profile.

## Is this better than preserving every reciprocal axis?

Yes in one important sense: it does not demand a second reciprocal angular pair and does not fight
the curved angular sector. It also works with all eight tested metrics containing nonzero cross
terms.

## Is it therefore the missing UDT principle?

Not yet. We supplied the clock/ruler plane and asked whether it could be consistently transported.
UDT has not yet been shown to create that spacetime plane globally, require its preservation, or
remove every twisting and directional-shear geometry that would violate the rule.

## Honest choice

```text
If the full UDT metric and seal derive an integrable, uniformly coupled two-plane:
    the unique projector connection is a strong frame-law candidate.
Otherwise:
    projector preservation is an attractive restriction imported into the metric, not derived from it.
```

No action, matter carrier, source, boundary, or mass was solved by this test.
