# Lay decision tree

## What did putting the background “on shell” do?

It replaced an arbitrary clock-dilation curve with a tightly limited family. In this small model,
the clock factor must be a cubic curve whose four constants obey one exact relationship.

That is real progress: the background is no longer something we are free to draw by hand.

## Did it choose the useful twist balance?

No. A legal cubic can make the ordinary-twist term positive in one region, zero at another point,
and negative elsewhere. So the local curvature equation alone does not decide whether the metric
twist is supported, neutral, or opposed there.

## Did the two-term geometric bridge disappear?

No. Whenever the cubic bends, the lower-order term remains alongside the always-positive
higher-order term. What remains missing is the rule that selects the cubic and the physical region.

## What false shortcut was removed?

Varying only the clock curve says “any cubic is allowed.” The full metric equation adds a transverse
constraint. For example, `y=1+r^2` passes the one-function equation but fails the full tensor
equation. This confirms that UDT cannot be safely reduced to one attractive scalar equation before
the other metric sectors are checked.

## Where is selection most likely hiding now?

In the pieces deliberately absent from this flat local tile:

- the intrinsic angular curvature and transverse size/shear;
- the finite-cell caps and boundary completion;
- the whole-universe bootstrap that chooses one globally admissible solution.

The next useful calculation should couple those angular/global obligations to the reciprocal clock
factor. More local polynomial searching or coefficient fitting would not answer the selector
question.
