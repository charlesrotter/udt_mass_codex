# Lay decision tree

## What the calculation separated

There are two different coordinate frames in play:

1. the natural pair of UDT directions on which reciprocal dilation acts simply; and
2. the physical clock-and-ruler frame in which a local observer measures light at `c` in opposite
   directions.

The current foundation gives us the first pair and the observed calibration `c`. It does not yet say
that those two pairs are the same pair of axes.

## If they are declared to be the same

The local light cone must be untilted in the reciprocal coordinates. Exact algebra then makes the
cross-term vanish. But the mixed metric needs that cross-term to have one time direction and one
space direction while also obeying the spatial reciprocal mirror. The mixed family is therefore
ruled out under this extra declaration.

That is a valid conditional result, but the declaration is not currently a UDT theorem.

## If only an ordinary local measuring frame is required

The mixed metric can be put exactly into the familiar local clock/ruler form. Light then has the two
local slopes `+c` and `-c`. In that measuring frame the reciprocal dilation no longer acts separately
on the clock and ruler axes; it mixes them.

Thus the observed value of `c` and the mixed reciprocal geometry can coexist. This does not choose a
unique mixed metric.

## Is the leftover number just notation?

No. A scalar built from the metric and reciprocal operator is unchanged when both are transformed
together, and that scalar reconstructs the mixing number `mu`. Two exact examples, `mu=4` and
`mu=9`, have different scalar values. No honest change of basis turns one pair into the other.

## The present fork

- If UDT derives that the reciprocal axes are the physical orthogonal clock/ruler axes, the mixed
  family dies.
- If UDT derives a nontrivial map from reciprocal axes to measured axes, that map must explain or
  select `mu`.
- If neither is derived, `mu` and the physical soldering remain open.

The current evidence puts us in the third case.
