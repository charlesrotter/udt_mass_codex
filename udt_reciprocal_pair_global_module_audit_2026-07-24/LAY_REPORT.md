# Lay report

We asked whether the two equally favored angular directions at the
reciprocal balance point might be the missing two-part matter structure.

The answer is subtler.

The two directions form a complete coordinate pair. Together they span the
entire two-dimensional angular system already present in the metric. So we
did not discover a smaller hidden carrier. We discovered that, at the
balance point, the metric displays the whole angular system without
preferring either direction.

That special equality exists only at the balance surface. On either side,
one direction becomes shorter than the other. At a three-way junction there
are three equally good directions and three possible pairs. The pair
therefore cannot simply be carried through every geometry as “the two
chosen directions.”

There is still a promising conditional result. If the complete universe
supplies a genuine mirror operation that exchanges the two angular
directions, and if the angular geometry closes with the right two smooth
caps, the mirror has a shared circular motion. That circle acts on a
three-sphere exactly like a Hopf rotation, and its quotient is a sphere with
one unit of Hopf twisting.

This route does not require choosing either angular direction individually.
That is progress.

But the metric has not yet selected:

- which full angular mirror operation is present;
- whether the required caps occur;
- the global periods and orientation;
- a physical field taking values on the quotient sphere; or
- the action that would make such a field into matter.

One exact curiosity may matter later: the mirror-shared and
mirror-opposite integer directions miss half of the original integer
lattice when simply added together. There is a residual two-way parity
structure. We recorded it without assigning it particle meaning.

In one sentence: the metric naturally contains the full angular orchestra,
and a conditional Hopf stage can be built from the orchestra as a whole,
but UDT has not yet supplied the concert hall, the global mirror, or the
rule that turns the geometry into matter.
