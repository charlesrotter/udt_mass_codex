# Lay report

## The good news

You were right that this is light-cone work and Lorentzian in nature.

Inside the WR-L metric, every local observer builds clocks and rulers from
the same one-time/one-distance geometry. The only continuous way to tilt
those clocks and rulers while preserving that geometry is the familiar
Lorentz-shaped transformation. We did not assume special relativity to get
it; it falls out of preserving the UDT metric at one event.

Every such observer agrees on the local light cone, and locally measured
light still has speed `c_E`.

## Why distant light appears to slow

In the static chart, the coordinate speed is

```text
c_E times the lapse A.
```

As the `X` horizon is approached, `A` approaches zero, so the coordinate
speed approaches zero. But the local clock slows and the local ruler
stretches in exactly the compensating way. A local static measurement still
returns `c_E`.

The optical distance to the horizon is infinite. A static observer can
never remain static all the way there; the acceleration required to hold
position grows without bound.

## The important correction

The `X` surface is a horizon, not automatically an absolute wall.

The metric can be rewritten in a perfectly regular crossing chart. Light
and timelike paths can cross the surface in that chart. So the following
two statements are different:

- a static observer never reaches the horizon in static clock/optical
  coordinates;
- no possible observer can cross it.

The metric proves the first and does not prove the second.

This makes `Xmax` somewhat less like the speed `c` than we hoped. `c` is a
limit on the direction a massive object can take at every event. A horizon
is a surface that an observer can cross. UDT would need a global boundary,
identification, or observer-centered horizon structure to turn it into a
universal positional limit.

## Where the angular sector enters

The most useful new result is that the radial and clock sectors are not the
problem. They transform together beautifully.

The surrounding angular geometry—the size and structure of the sphere
around the observer—does not follow the same scaling under the old
positional re-centering. It blocks the earlier global frame
transformation.

In orchestra language: the clock and radial instruments are already in
tune. The angular ensemble is playing a different transformation rule.
Until the complete score joins them, we have local Lorentzian reciprocity
but not full observer-centered reciprocity for the universe.

That is a concrete missing object, not another vague “underdetermined”
result.
