# Lay report

We removed the hidden linkage that made all parts of the metric move together.

Imagine the metric as having ten geometric controls. The earlier map turned all ten with one common
handle. That showed what happened when everything moved together, but it could not tell us whether
the controls were genuinely independent.

This time each control received its own handle. `phi` received an eleventh handle that was not
connected to the metric. Across 480 deliberately varied configurations, all ten metric handles were
independent when we counted not just the value of the metric at a point, but also how it changes and
curves around that point. The `phi` handle was independently verified as a separate direction.

At one special sample point, every chosen polynomial happens to equal zero. Looking only at the
metric's value there makes the ten handles appear to do nothing. Looking at the slopes and curvatures
around that point reveals all ten directions. An independent symbolic calculation confirmed this;
it is exactly why local values alone are an incomplete map.

The most useful new contrast is that individual controls have different geometric footprints:

- Changing only `phi` leaves the metric unchanged.
- Changing one metric control at a time creates curvature, but not the fully mixed curvature seen
  when many controls move together.
- The screen-shape controls create shear.
- The shift controls create both shear and twist.
- Broad multi-control combinations activate the full sampled curvature, shear, and twist pattern.

That is a real clarification of what the complete metric is doing in this local family. It is not a
law of nature yet. We have mapped independent geometric directions; we have not shown which
configurations are physical solutions because UDT's complete dynamical law remains open.
