# Lay report

Your instinct was right: we were treating something already present as
though it still had to be invented.

The two founding ideas already tell UDT how to compare its clock side and
ruler side between two ordered positions. One side shrinks by exactly the
amount the other grows. Reversing the observer order reverses the
transformation. Chaining two comparisons gives the same answer as performing
the combined comparison.

When the same transformation is viewed in a balanced clock-and-space basis,
it has exactly the mathematical form of a Lorentz-type boost. We did not
borrow that boost from Einsteinian mechanics; it was already hidden inside
the reciprocal operator.

We also caught an important distinction. In the simplest UDT metric, the
coordinates used to write clocks and rulers acquire this reciprocal scaling
when carried from one place to another. But the physical orthonormal
measuring frame is carried into the other local physical frame without a
spatial boost. Coordinate scaling alone therefore does not tell us what two
actual observers mutually see on their clocks.

So the missing part is smaller than we thought:

- UDT already has the comparison machine.
- It has not yet told us exactly what number to feed that machine for every
  pair of observers in the complete centerless universe.
- It has also not completely specified how their clock readings are paired
  when angular geometry, motion, curvature, and multiple routes matter.

Paths are therefore not needed to create the basic clock operator. They
matter later, when the complete metric supplies the pair's depth and carries
the angular parts of the observers' frames.

The next clean question is whether UDT's positional depth is a truly
two-observer quantity, an observer-based family of depth charts, or a
difference of one global local field. Those possibilities agree in simple
one-dimensional examples but differ sharply in a closed universe with no
center.
