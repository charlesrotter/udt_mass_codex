# Lay report — the raw orchestra-volume map

The earlier work identified eleven independent controls: ten parts of the metric and `phi`. It also
showed that one instrument at a time produces only part of the geometry, while broad combinations
activate curvature, shear, twist, and interaction between the depth and angular sectors.

One raw question was still unanswered: was that full effect peculiar to a few carefully chosen
chords?

We tested it in two ways. First, we selected 64 deliberately scattered points inside the space of
all eleven control settings. Second, we chose sixteen very different chords and raised their volume
gradually from silence through five levels. Each setting was heard, mathematically, in eight local
contexts. Nothing was rejected for sounding wrong.

The sampled answer was unusually uniform. The exact silent setting was flat. Every sampled nonzero setting—
all 1,152 of them—activated the same complete *kind* of local geometry: full sampled curvature,
two independent shear directions, twist, and mixing between the main and angular parts of the
metric. Turning a chord up changed the numerical strength of those effects, but did not change that
basic inventory after the first nonzero step.

That does not mean we found the laws of physics—or that this behavior has any known frequency in the
unsampled continuum. We have shown only that the full geometric orchestra was not confined to the
few sign-corner chords previously tested. We have not found the score that tells the instruments
what to play, which performances are actual solutions, or which one represents our universe.

The preregistered raw amplitude tile is complete, so we may now begin organizing the instruments
into structural ensembles without claiming that all useful amplitude mapping is finished. Other
large mapping tasks remain—different mathematical charts, richer functions, global boundaries, and
real time-dependent equations—and unsampled amplitudes can still contain other behavior.
