# Lay report

We found a real piece of the complete metric's internal machinery.

Imagine the metric has six basic ways an observer frame can change:

- three ways to change motion, one for each spatial direction; and
- three ways to rotate.

On the branch where `dphi` points in a time direction, the earlier
three-plus-three structure is exactly this division: three
"change-of-motion" pieces and three rotation pieces.

The important result is that these are not independent instruments. If
you combine changes of motion in two different directions, their
mathematical commutator produces a rotation in the third direction. The
exact example used here combines the x and y directions and produces a
rotation in the x-y screen, around the z direction.

So the angular sector is not something we had to invent to repair the
reciprocal sector. It is already required when the complete metric allows
reciprocal observer changes in more than one direction.

There is also a useful limit on that discovery.

The simple reciprocal exponential works perfectly along one selected
direction: one null direction gains the factor `exp(phi)` while the
opposite one gets `exp(-phi)`. But no nontrivial single scalar rule can
represent the entire observer-frame group. Once changes point in
different directions, order matters and rotations appear.

The earlier `dphi` weighting does divide the six pieces into two groups of
three, but applying opposite exponential weights to those groups is not
itself a legal observer-frame transformation except when the weight is
trivial. Therefore:

- the underlying three-plus-three geometry joins exactly;
- the angular rotation emerges naturally from it;
- the reciprocal weighting remains a distinct metric-field operation;
- and metric `phi` has not been shown to equal observer rapidity.

This is promising because it replaces two apparently separate clues with
one coherent geometric structure. It does not yet say why the universe
chooses the required timelike `dphi` branch, how the structure moves
through a whole finite cell, or how matter and dynamics emerge.
