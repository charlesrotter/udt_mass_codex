# Lay report

We asked a very specific question:

> Does the complete UDT metric itself make the angular part keep a constant
> relative area and change shape at exactly the same unit rate as the
> reciprocal clock/ruler part?

The answer is **not globally—not from the rules currently registered**. But
we did find one real piece of the answer.

The angular metric has two independent motions:

1. its overall two-dimensional area can expand or contract; and
2. its shape can stretch one way while squeezing the other.

The desired reciprocal pattern requires the first motion to be zero and the
second to have exactly unit strength.

At a mirrored finite-cell boundary, the metric itself forces the first
motion to stop: the angular area is stationary relative to the clock/ruler
part exactly at the mirror. That is a genuine geometric result.

The mirror does not determine the strength of the shape change. Depending
on how the angular directions are reflected, that strength is either zero or
free. Nothing already registered says the free value must be one.

We also constructed smooth complete-metric deformations that:

- leave the cell endpoints and their first two derivatives unchanged;
- retain nonzero clock/ruler–angular cross terms;
- independently alter angular area, fixed-axis stretching, and rotating-axis
  stretching inside the cell.

So boundary regularity alone cannot secretly be fixing the interior.

In plain terms, we have found half of a possible boundary instruction, not
the complete law:

```text
At the mirror: do not change relative angular area.
```

Still missing is the metric-native rule that says:

```text
Throughout the cell: keep that area relation,
stretch/squeeze with unit strength,
and transport the directions consistently.
```

We have made the open problem smaller and more exact without adding a new
mechanism. The next legitimate step is to look for that bulk propagation or
normalization law in the complete metric—not to assume it.
