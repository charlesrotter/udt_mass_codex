# Lay Report

We asked a simple but important question: when the metric seems to show several sectors playing
together, is that real geometry, or did our chosen coordinates make it look that way?

The answer has two layers.

The robust layer is the full geometry. We rewrote every one of the 6,144 metric configurations in
twelve different ways—changing coordinates, boosting and rotating the measuring frame, and even
letting those frame changes vary locally. The curvature “orchestra” stayed the same. Its individual
numbers moved between components, much as the north/east components of a wind change when you rotate
a map, but the geometric wind itself did not change.

The fragile layer is how we divide that orchestra into named sections. If we keep the same two
directions as the base and the same two as the screen, the named slot, shear, and twist patterns stay
the same. But if we mix those directions and then declare the new first two coordinates to be the
base and the last two to be the screen, many new-looking interactions appear. Nothing new happened to
the metric or curvature. We changed which instruments we grouped together.

One deliberately extreme relabeling put the time direction inside the part that was required to be a
positive spatial screen. The four-dimensional geometry remained perfectly valid, but that particular
base/screen description stopped being valid. We kept all those cases in the atlas as “split
undefined,” rather than throwing them away.

So the useful insight is:

> The symphony in the full curvature is real within this atlas. The assignment of notes to “base,”
> “screen,” “shear,” and “twist” is not yet intrinsic.

This sharpens the next conceptual question. UDT would need to supply a geometric projector or pair of
distributions that identifies the split without relying on coordinate position or coframe row names.
The atlas did not derive that selector, and it did not choose an action or matter law.
