# Lay Decision Tree

## What did we learn?

We found a very simple obstruction hiding underneath the complicated geometry.

Einsteinian `c` tells us how to convert time into distance. `G` tells us how to compare mass with
distance. Together they give a conversion rate—mass per distance—but they do not provide one actual
distance.

It is like knowing that a map uses ten pounds of mass for every mile of size without being told
whether the map represents one mile, a million miles, or a billion light-years.

## Don't the mass equation and the Xmax equation solve one another?

No. At present they say the same thing in reverse:

- size is proportional to mass;
- mass is proportional to size.

If their constants agree, every enlarged or shrunken copy still works. If their constants disagree,
there is no nonzero solution. They do not select one size.

## Doesn't density add a third fact?

Not when density is calculated from that same mass and that same volume. Then it is bookkeeping, not
new information. It becomes new information only if UDT independently derives a particular physical
density that matter must have.

## What does Xmax reciprocity do?

It still does something important: it tells us where something lies as a fraction of the universe's
maximum reach and how such positional comparisons combine.

But every fraction remains the same if the whole universe is enlarged. Reciprocity organizes the
map; it does not print the missing scale bar.

## What kind of discovery could supply the scale bar?

The completed theory would have to produce one genuinely independent dimensional fact—for example a
native matter density, boundary eigenvalue, period, or other scale-bearing quantity. That fact
cannot be defined from the mass and size it is supposed to determine.

This does not mean inventing another constant. It means that if the metric, matter, or boundary
really generates such a quantity, we must expose exactly how. If it does not, UDT may select only
relative structure and compactness while the absolute size remains observationally calibrated.

## Would that finish the metric?

Not by itself. Fixing the universe's overall size still leaves the parent audit's interior
common-scale family. A second join must tell all interior clocks, radial rulers, and angular rulers
how the global scale is realized.

## Where should we look next?

Not in another rearrangement of `M`, `Xmax`, `c`, and `G`; dimensional analysis has exhausted that
route. The honest next question is whether the metric's complete matter/boundary/bootstrap system
contains an additional native scale-bearing object—and, if so, whether the same equation fixes the
interior coframe rather than only the outer ruler.
