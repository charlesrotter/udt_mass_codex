# Lay decision tree

## What was hiding in plain sight

The finite-cell mirror already tells us more than we had credited it with.

At the mirror surface, one combination of the two reciprocal directions stays unchanged and the
other reverses. In the mixed Lorentz metric those two combinations are automatically perpendicular.
Calling the mirror spatial then tells us which is the clock direction and which is the radial
direction. Normalizing their lengths gives the ordinary local clock/ruler frame. The same operation
with an overall minus sign is the corresponding time reversal.

So the base clock/ruler join is not wholly missing. It is conditionally present at the seal.

## What it does not choose

The mirror chooses the correct sign family, but it does not choose the remaining amount of mixing,
called `mu`. Two exact complete-metric examples with `mu=4` and `mu=9` obey the same mirror, angular
metric, nonzero angular coupling, signature, orientation, and local `c` convention.

Common-scale normalization and even fixing the full determinant do not distinguish them.

## Why the angular sector still matters

The reciprocal base can be extended through the angular directions in several inequivalent ways.
Once one such extension is supplied, the metric can construct an orthogonal transverse plane even
when the base and angular coordinates are mixed. But UDT has not yet selected which complete
angular, normal, and time-dependent mirror action the universe uses.

That choice and its global continuation could still select `mu`. The current local rules do not.

## Where we stand

- Local base soldering at the finite-cell seal: conditionally derived.
- Correct spatial-versus-temporal sign branch: conditionally derived.
- Magnitude `mu`: genuinely open.
- Complete angular/normal/time-on lift: open.
- Global continuation and bootstrap selection: open.

The missing object is smaller than before: not an entirely new clock/ruler rule, but the complete
lift and global equation that extend the seal-local construction through the whole metric.
