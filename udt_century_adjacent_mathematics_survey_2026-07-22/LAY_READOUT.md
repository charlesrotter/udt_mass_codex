# Lay Readout

Yes—the historical survey was worth doing.

It did not uncover a century-old equation that we can simply plug into UDT. What it uncovered is a
clean way to stop wandering.

Imagine we are looking for a switch hidden inside the metric. There are only two broad places that
switch can live:

1. **Locally:** every small patch of the metric contains the same rule. If so, established
   mathematics says the rule must be built from a finite amount of local metric information—roughly,
   the metric and some finite number of its derivatives. Once we say what kind of answer the switch
   produces, we can make a complete parts list and check every allowed part. That search can end.
2. **Globally:** no small patch contains the decision. The decision only appears when the entire
   finite universe is assembled—how its edges join, what happens at the seal, how the whole solution
   balances itself, or how one global pattern feeds back into itself. If the local parts list is
   empty, we will know to stop drilling locally and look only at the whole construction.

The survey also sharpened our “orchestra” picture. Finding an interesting curvature direction at
one point is like hearing one clean note. It does not prove that the same instrument remains distinct
as the music moves through the whole piece. The right test follows curvature and its changes together
and asks whether any subspace remains intact under all of them. Mathematicians call the resulting
structure holonomy. We had tested an important first slice; we had not yet performed this complete
persistence test.

Several tempting shortcuts were also ruled out:

- A special-looking scale or spectrum does not uniquely determine a shape.
- A fixed-point theorem cannot create the feedback rule that it needs to test.
- A topological Hopf pattern does not create its own carrier or energy law.
- A useful geometric flow is not automatically physical time.
- A local equation does not automatically possess a global action.
- A boundary flux is not automatically mass.

So the main gain is conceptual contrast. The immediate scientific work should be a paired audit:

1. make a complete census of every local selector the full UDT metric could naturally contain;
2. test whether the full curvature-and-derivative orchestra preserves any genuine geometric
   substructure across a finite cell.

If both come back empty in the generic metric, that is not another inconclusive result. It would be
a strong positive direction marker: the missing UDT principle must act on the whole finite universe,
not point by point. If either succeeds, we will have an actual metric-derived building block to carry
forward into global assembly.

Nothing in the survey changes UDT's existing scientific claims, and nothing external was imported
as UDT physics.
