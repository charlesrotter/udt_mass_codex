# Lay report — what the time-live metric actually supplied

The complete spherical metric did reveal something new, but it is narrower and cleaner than a new
law of physics.

Every symmetry sphere has a measurable area. From that area the metric defines an **areal radius**:
the radius an ordinary sphere with that area would have. The direction in spacetime along which this
areal radius changes, together with its metric-perpendicular partner, supplies a natural local pair
of directions. In an ordinary branch, one is space-directed and the other is time-directed. This is
not a preferred observer imposed on the laws; it is structure carried by a particular spherical
geometry, much as the center of a spherical room is supplied by the room rather than by a universal
law favoring that location.

The exact scalar controlling the pair is called `X`. In the founded static metric it reduces to the
familiar reciprocal factor:

```text
X = exp(-2 phi).
```

The full time-dependent metric admits three exact possibilities:

- `X>0`: areal radius points spatially and its perpendicular partner points temporally;
- `X=0`: the two directions meet on a single null direction; and
- `X<0`: their jobs exchange—areal radius is time-directed and its partner is space-directed.

That is a real metric-native role exchange. It is **not** a flip of the whole spacetime signature,
not a breakdown of observational-frame reciprocity, and not yet a microscopic transition. The full
metric remains Lorentzian throughout every regular member of the family. Nor does `X=0` alone prove
an event horizon, physical edge, CMB surface, or particle boundary.

The angular sector also reproduces the earlier center result in invariant language: a regular
spherical center must approach `X=1` rapidly enough. In the static branch this is exactly the return
to `phi=0` found in the preceding audit.

## The important missing piece is now precise

Knowing `X` and the two local directions still does not determine the complete time-dependent
metric. An additional positive function, denoted `F(T,R)`, survives. In plain language, the metric
has told us which way each local clock points, but not the complete geometric rule that normalizes
and threads those clocks from one sphere to the next.

This is not merely a coordinate nuisance. Two exact metrics can have the same `X=1` and the same
local areal directions while different radial dependence of `F` gives different spacetime
curvature. Therefore `phi`, if identified with `-log(X)/2` on the positive branch, does not contain
the whole time-live geometry.
